Sight

My goal with this project was to write some algorithms that produce interesting visuals in real time in response to sound. The underlying theory and math behind analyzing sound is somewhat beyond me, but that is where a framework like p5.js comes in handy. The framework takes care of a lot of the complicated stuff so that you can instead focus on exploring and expressing ideas.

The basic process involves analyzing a sound's waveform and extracting data points of interest, like amplitude and bass, mid, and treble ranges, and then using those values to animate changes to a visual that is being rendered on a screen. I found three amazing code examples to use as the starting points for my visualization engine.

As usual, I was overly ambitious with my project. I wanted each visualization to be capable of producing interesting results regardless of what kind of song was being played. On top of that I wanted them to work for mic input. And I also wanted the color modes to always produce interesting combinations. It was challenging, and took a lot of trial and error to get something looked decent most of the time. Here's a screenshot of the settings panel. I tried to keep it as minimal as possible. You can either upload your own mp3, use mic input if you are on a compatible browser, or play the song I composed. There's an option to use a single visualization or have the app randomly cycle between all of them. The "Intensity" slider provides a way to influence the energy of the visualization.

Sound

Echo Opera is a former colleague of mine from frog. I don't know why, but his tweet about the MPC One inspired me to try and create a song. Just to be clear, he's an actual and brilliant musician, I am not! Here's a photo of my humble setup.

My primary goal in creating a song was to learn how to use the MPC One and to make something to go along with my sound visualizer app. I discovered a lovely song about a sailor by Russian actor and singer Mark Bernes on Radiooooo and used vocal clips and a piano snippet to establish the tone of the song. I created the rest of the instrumentation and song structure in the process of learning the MPC One.
